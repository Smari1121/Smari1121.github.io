Laxmi Vilas Palace: Vadodara's Crown Jewel

If you’ve grown up in Vadodara like I have, there’s one place that always stands larger than life—both literally and emotionally—the Laxmi Vilas Palace. It's not just a monument or a tourist stop; it's like that proud, towering elder in the family whose presence quietly shapes the soul of the city.
Built in 1890 by Maharaja Sayajirao Gaekwad III, the palace was designed to feel more like a home than a fortress, although the scale of it would make you think otherwise. Four times the size of Buckingham Palace (yep, that’s not an exaggeration), it remains one of the largest private residences in the world. The first time I stood in front of it as a kid, I remember my neck craning all the way back, eyes wide, wondering how something so majestic could belong to the same Vadodara I saw every day.
The Indo-Saracenic architecture, the sprawling gardens, the way the palace seems to glow at golden hour—there’s something cinematic about it, but also deeply comforting. Maybe it’s because it’s always been there. No matter how much the city evolves, Laxmi Vilas stands still, like a quiet witness to our growing up years.
What makes this place even more special is its connection to art and culture. Inside, the palace holds a treasure trove of paintings by Raja Ravi Varma—India’s very own master of portraiture. I remember peeking in during a heritage walk, feeling like I was stepping into a time capsule where royalty, artistry, and legacy sat in quiet conversation. And yet, despite all this opulence, the place never feels alienating. It feels like it belongs to the people of Vadodara, not just the Gaekwad family.
Even the grounds are steeped in stories. There’s a golf course, a museum, and often events that subtly remind you that history isn’t always in the past—it’s something you live alongside. The first time I saw the palace lit up during a cultural event, it felt like time had folded—like the Vadodara of yesteryears had joined hands with the present.
For me, it’s more than history or architecture. It’s the quiet nostalgia of school field trips, the curious joy of retelling facts to visiting friends, the pride of saying, “That’s in my city.” Whenever I feel far from home, mentally or physically, a picture of Laxmi Vilas flashes in my head—solid, grand, familiar. It reminds me that roots can be majestic too.
So, if you ever visit Vadodara—or if you’re from here and somehow haven’t truly seen it—go to Laxmi Vilas Palace not just with your camera, but with your time. Let it speak to you slowly. It has a lot to say.
